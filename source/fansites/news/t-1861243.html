Battle.net Mobile Authenticator Updated
The Battle.net Mobile Authenticator app has recently been updated for both iOS and Android, and it’s been rebuilt from the ground up to make it faster, more efficient, and easier to use. You’ll also notice some extra shine and polish, including a brand-new design that displays a new piece of art from one of Blizzard’s games every time you load a code.

Android
Players using the iOS version will also now be able to use iCloud to make it easier than ever to back up and restore your Authenticator, streamlining the setup process whenever you need to recover or switch to a new device.

Android phones can be hacked without the user being aware simply by sending it a text message.

IPhones are secure, but Droid is not. Just need the phone number and you can pwn a Droid.

Saying that iOS is more secure than Android is like saying that driving sports cars result in more fatal crashes than pickup trucks. Yes, that may be statistically true (or not), but correlation does not equal causation. The fatality count has way more to do with the psychology of sports car owners (the driving habits of the kinds of people who buy sports cars) and the physics of the situation (you're more likely to survive a crash in a truck).

So yes, while you see a larger number of hacked devices that are Android over iOS, the cause isn't in the operating system being inherently less secure. The culprit is far more likely that Android users tend to be more "power users" than iOS device owners, and are more likely to root and sideload apps than iOS owners are to jailbreak their devices. Since most sources of smartphone hacks are from malware that are sideloaded into devices, you can see why this might cause the statistics to show more hacked Android phones. Another culprit is Android's distributed update system. 3rd party manufacturers often lag behind in core updates while Apple has the ability to push updates to all their customers at once, thus if you aren't using vanilla Android, you may have problems picking up regular security patches. There has actually been significantly more vulnerabilities found in iOS than Android, something on the order of about 30%.

At the end of the day, Android gives you more rope to hang yourself with, the cost of giving you more freedom to do whatever you want with your phone. If you choose to use one, then it's on you to be more tech-savvy and security minded; blaming Android for having more hacks is somewhat unfair. If you want something that is is mindlessly secure, then iOS may be a better choice for you and there's nothing wrong with that. Just be aware that if you choose to jailbreak and sideload apps, then you are willingly giving up that security layer.

Oh, and as an aside? iPhones have similar vulnerabilities that only require a text message to be received and not even read to trigger. (I can't seem to post URLs, but you can easily find the news article by searching for "iphone hacked by text message".) Yes, I'm aware that crashing a phone isn't the same as compromising its security, but they're both news points from this year and it's food for thought.
