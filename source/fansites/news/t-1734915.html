Join the Party at BlizzCon 2015 November 6 and 7
You’ve been summoned to Southern California for BlizzCon 2015! Blizzard’s ninth epic gaming convention and community celebration is returning to the Anaheim Convention Center on Friday, November 6 and Saturday, November 7, 2015. Form a party with your fellow members of the Blizzard community and get ready for two days of in-depth discussion panels with our developers, hands-on play time with the latest Blizzard games, and intense eSports tournaments featuring top pro gamers from around the world.

BlizzCon 2015 tickets will go on sale in two batches on Wednesday, April 15 at 7 p.m. PT and Saturday, April 18 at 10 a.m. PT through the online event ticketing service Eventbrite (http://blizz.ly/1MueS10), priced at $199 each (plus applicable taxes and fees). Check out the BlizzCon Ticket webpage (http://blizz.ly/1HEISEE) for all the details.

In addition, we’re once again planning a special pre-BlizzCon Benefit Dinner the evening of Thursday, November 5 in support of Children’s Hospital of Orange County (CHOC) (http://blizz.ly/1E6EuKN), where guests will have a chance to meet and chat with developers, artists, community team representatives, and others from Blizzard. Attendance to this special event will be limited, and we’ll have more information on ticket pricing and availability at a later date.

If you can’t make it to the show in person, you’ll be able to watch from home with the BlizzCon Virtual Ticket, offering comprehensive live coverage of the event via a multichannel Internet stream. Further details on the Virtual Ticket, including pricing, availability, and programming information, will be announced at a later date.

See the full press release (http://blizz.ly/1BxUpTX) or check out the BlizzCon Ticket page for more information, and be sure to visit www.blizzcon2015.eventbrite.com at the dates and times above for a chance to purchase a ticket to the show. We hope to see you there!

Ummm, you do know that their is an actual limit to the number of people you can fit inside the.... Indoor Convention Center... right?

Fire codes limit that - but it's also calculated on the flow of people in and out all day. Most conventions have people that show up, take an hour or two to look around, and then leave. Shows like car shows, the old MacWorld shows, or the music industry convention that's held at the same place Blizzcon is (NAMM) don't expect people to show up and stay all day. Blizzcon is a destination - you go, and stay all day, both days. That's going to limit how many people you can cycle in and out every day.

http://www.anaheimconventioncenter.com/Article.aspx?id=42 shows the capacities of the halls. Note that you can't just add up the numbers and sell that many tickets, it's a bit more complicated than that, you have to account for the space taken up by booths, cordons, shops, concessions, backstage, staging, support areas like where the servers are, the TV production stuff, etc.

30-40k is about right for a crowd that's not going to leave for two days. The only way they could expand is to go to a bigger convention center, like the LA Convention Center, but moving there would have it's own issues. E3 is held there, so they have experience with gaming shows, but parking and accommodations and cost are going to be an issue.

Scaling events like this is vastly more complicated than most people think. I've been part of planning for corporate events at this scale, it can take a team of people a year or more to put a show as big as Blizzcon on. Doubling the size is a lot more work than you could ever know, unless you've done it. Every aspect changes with a doubling of attendees. (adding 10% more wouldn't be worth the time and effort, and your choices past the Anaheim Convention Center are extremely limited) I'm sure the team at Blizzard who does the show would love to get more people in to enjoy it, but have a budget that they have to consider, and the complications of moving the show further from their offices, dealing with downtown LA, and adding many layers of complexity, to an already complex issue. Blizzcon in Anaheim works, and if they could just throw money at it to sell more tickets, they would have done so. They could add more days, but that makes booking the center more complicated and expensive - but I'm surprised they haven't added a day by now.

The one thing they haven't done, which surprises me, but in some ways doesn't, is to have an east coast show one year, a west coast the next, and have a EU show as well. Spread the ticket sales out regionally, make it easier for other regions to attend. Who knows, if they remain as profitable as they have been, and as popular, they may do that eventually.
