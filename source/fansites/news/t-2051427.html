They're saying they were working on faster expansions so all their eggs were in the expansion basket and no work was done on the live game, if that were fucking true then the expansions would have come out sooner.
First of all, yes, yearly expansions has been a goal since Burning Crusade:

https://www.engadget.com/2007/03/16/an-expansion-a-year-blizzard-says-well-try/

It has never worked out and has never been a particularily good strategy, but they clearly wanted to try and create a short patch cycle followed quickly by an expansion in order to provide a steady stream of new content (and box sales revenue).


Second, just because all your eggs are in one basket doesn't mean they'll hatch faster. Some things take time. Designing, developing, and testing an entire expansion's worth of content takes a lot of time, and it takes a lot of talented people. Just because they spend more money or hire more people doesn't mean they can finish the work sooner, since everything is still subject to a workflow with bottlenecks. The best you can really hope for is that it'll allow you to develop more content in parallel.

As a metaphor, imagine you want to build a cabinet. Maybe if you hire two, or even three people, you can get it built quicker. However there's still a limit to the number of people who can feasibly work on a single cabinet at a time, and if you exceed that you get diminishing returns or even cause delays.


Something a lot of people also don't ever seem to consider is that the game's production values have also increased considerably with the release of each new batch of content. Models, rigs, animations, and textures have more detail. There's more voice acting, more music, more cutscenes. New systems are constantly being developed and added. As they increase the fidelity of the game and the quality of the art, it takes more talent and more time to complete everything. This is true industry-wide, but with WoW -- a 12-year-old game -- you can really see the difference over time.


I'm not saying you don't have the right to be pessimistic. Blizzard has promised an end to content droughts before, and has failed every time. However, it's worth showing at least a little optimism, given the fact that they've acknowledged the problem and are making a change to their design philosophy. Even if they are unable to deliver on the promise of shorter content gaps at the end of expansions, they've at least commited to providing a robust patch cycle for the expansion, so there should not be a shortage of content.
