3D printing must be making manufacturing companies take a big dump in their panties.

I highly doubt that. Manufacturing firms are geared up to deal with economies of scale and mass demand. I doubt a company that, let's say, makes hundreds of millions of aluminium cans a year, or a company that takes enormous bulk-orders for custom designs, gears up huge factory-floor scale machinery and has the power to not only keep costs low and maintain the equipment, but also has contracted distribution networks and advertising agencies...

...would feel remotely threatened that Jeff has a small 3D printer in his basement that he uses to produce the occasional thing. Even if you take an absolutely ridiculous and unrealistic scenario, and say that everyone owned a 3D printer and made cans to sell to coca-cola. Since we'd be buying in lower quantities, and due to economies of scale, our overhead would be a lot higher than a big firm that orders their materials in bulk. It's simply not economical to make more numerous smaller shipments to multiple smaller households or workshops, when you can make larger shipments to a much lower number of centralized factories. For it to be profitable to 3D printer owners, they'd consequently have to charge more per can. Why would Coca-Cola then buy cans at a premium price per can from us, when they could continue buying cans at a pittance from big factories? Not to mention the more people doing this, the harder it is to advertise and thus the more expensive advertising is.

If anything the big companies can pull 3D printing off far, far more efficiently than home-dwellers or hobbyists can. Rather than feeling threatened by the technology, they put it to far better use than home-dwellers ever could.
