Important - Adobe Flash Player Vulnerability
A critical vulnerability has been discovered in Adobe Flash Player 10.0.45.2 and Adobe Reader/Acrobat 9.x, and could potentially be used to target World of Warcraft players and accounts. The newest available version of Adobe Flash 10.1, Release Candidate 7 (available at http://labs.adobe.com/technologies/flashplayer10/), does not appear to contain this vulnerability, and we recommend that everyone upgrade their Flash player as soon as possible. Earlier versions of Adobe Reader and Acrobat, specifically version 8.x, do not appear to contain this vulnerability, either.

For more information, please visit Adobe.com: http://www.adobe.com/support/security/advisories/apsa10-01.html

Get an Authenticator if you haven't got one already. Get your very own guard dog and secure your account at the same time. Visit http://us.battle.net/security for more info!

I would also like to add that this is NOT a virus. The only way to protect yourself from this kind of vulnerability is to keep your system up-to-date in all ways, including Flash.

The advice "don't google them," seems a tad on the harsh side.

Here's how you use Google, folks:

Search for the terms you are interested in. In Firefox, this is accomplished most easily by typing "Ctrl-t <tab>" and then entering your search term(s) in the search widget. In Chrome, you want to just type "Ctrl-t" and your search term(s).

When the results come up, you see many things at once. At the top, you may see "Sponsored links". In general, you should ignore any links in this section (I do), but Google is very good about telling you what site you're going to visit when clicking one of these. You'll see 3 things in a sponsored link:

* Site name (can be misleading)
* URL (usually in green by default, below name)
* Descriptive text (just ad text, ignore)

If the site that you want is the one listed in the URL such as "mmo-champion.com" then it's safe to click the link, but you have to be REALLY careful to double-check the link name. It might be "mmo-chumpion.com" or something equally crafty. If you see a deceptive link name, feel free to report it to Google. They'll cancel people's ad campaigns for that faster than Blizzard boots gold farmers (a LOT faster).

Now, moving past sponsored links section, you have regular links. I just searched for "mmo champion" and got this:


MMO-Champion - World of Warcraft Guides and Raid Strategies
Jun 8, 2010 ... Articles and forums with game news and raiding strategies.
www.mmo-champion.com/ - 11 minutes ago - Cached - Similar
Forum / Blue Tracker
Talent Calculator / Loot List
World of Warcraft Guides and Raid / Lord Jaraxxus
Raid Composition

More results from mmo-champion.com »

Again, there are several sections:

* Name (just a page title, can be misleading)
* Description (snippet of page, can be misleading)
* URL (e.g. "www.mmo-champion.com/", read this carefully)
* Some sites have a list of common links
* At the bottom you may see "more results from"...

The most critical part is the URL. Read it carefully, and if it's the site you expected to see results from, then it's safe to visit. If it's a misleading name like "mmo-chumpion.com" then I recommend not visiting it. In that case, they've already proven to be less than trustworthy, so there's no need to give them a second chance.

The list of common links is safe if you trust the site listed in the URL. They will be links to the same site, but to sub-pages linked to from the page in the main result. For example, a Google search for MMO Champion shows items like "Forum" and "Talent calculator" that go to the respective sections of the site.

In fact, these sub-section links are partially controlled by MMO Champion. Their main page has special code that tells Google which links are important for visiting sub-sections and Google remembers this for later. They then provide the list of the most commonly visited sub-sections in search results.

And there you have it. That's how you safely use Google search. Happy searching.

Implementations will have vulnerabilities, certainly, but that's not the same thing. This is important because it's a question of scale. When Flash Player has a vulnerability it mean that Flash has a vulnerability, the product and the standard are essentially the same thing. When I.E. 10 has an HTML5 related vulnerability, that does not per se indicate that Safari 6, Firefox 5, or Chrome 4 have the same vulnerability.
